About Face (Digital Products)

About designing interactive digital products

Visual design focuses on the static-ness of information, where as interactive products require a "communication" or a relationship between the user and the product

User experience contains these 3 elements.

Interaction design focuses on the design of behavior but also is concerned with how that behavior relates to form and content

Design process for digital products - Goal Directed Design Process

What to focus on?

  1. Understand the desires, needs, motivations and context of people using the products
  1. Understand the business, technical and domain opportunities, requirements and contsraints
  1. Using this knowledge as a foundation for plans to create products whose form, content, and behavior are useful, usable, and desirable, as well as economically viable and technically feasible

AIM: To get into the mind of the user. We need to do whatever it takes to get there.

There are 2 types of data we can gather when trying to get into the mind of the users

  • First is quantative data like how many downloads, how many members per month, how many sales etc.
  • Second is qualitative data like an interview recording of a potential user explaining why he chose product A vs product B, talks about his goals and everyday life etc.

A bunch of research methods that are effective

  • Stakeholder Interviews - Try and answer the following (with Biz folks mainly)
    • What is the product?
    • Who will/does use it?
    • What do your users need most?
    • Biggest competitors?
  • Literature Review - Google/Web search, existing content
  • Competitive Analysis
  • SME Interviews - Expert users, gather domain knowledge
  • Customer Interviews - goals, painpoints, decision process, domain vocabulary
  • Observations - See how they do things rather than what they say
  • Contextual Inquiry - Set the context, conduct in phases
    • initial phases - goal, motivation oriented
    • later phases - task, granular detailed oriented
  • Usability Testing - primarily to test interaction specific, with an artifact, most effective when trying to re-design something
  • Card Sorting - usually to understand how a user sorts categories of information
  • Task Analysis - why they are doing a specific task

A systematic step-by-step approach to bridge the gap between the user and the product

  • It starts from personal goals. Demarcate b/w personal and business goals. Eg: We think clerk's goals are to process payroll efficiently - but that's actually the employer's goal. The clerk's goal could be "being competant enough and not to replaced by someone else"
  • Demarcate b/w goals, tasks and activities. A goal is an expectation of an end condition, whereas both activities and tasks are intermediate steps (at different levels of organization) that help someone to reach a goal or set of goals.
  • D Norman says: Activies = Tasks + (Actions). Actions ‚â§ Tasks i.e Task = [Action 1 + Action 2 + ...]. Task based design is incorrect & incomplete.
Why understand goals? It helps know aspirations and motivations + the situation under which the person did something i.e. helps understand "why" they did what they did.
  • Conduct research to understand how the users are achieving their goals right now (personal + biz goals)
  • Should showcase how the system, object or artefact that the user is GOING to interact with - works. It's a representation of how the system works. Eg: API of an application, Architecture of a software (flow diagram)
  • List of features that is allowed by the system
  • A person's perception of how something works. Eg: A movie-goer doesn't need to know how a projector works, they think it's just series of pictures showcased really fast.
  • Interfaces should be based on user mental models rather than system models
  • State the goals of the users & their perceptions

  • Designer's job is to bring the system model as close as possible to the user's mental model

  • Find out the qualitative and quantitative data after research as much as possible

‚úçÔ∏è STEP 2 - We've conducted research, now what?

  • Model for WHO - Build a persona(s) that includes
    • Goals
    • Motivations, Needs, Intrests
    • Pain Points
    • Characteristics - map interview subjects (from step 1) into behavioural variables. Eg: Service oriented vs Price oriented. Necessity-only vs entertainment
  • Prioritise on a primary persona

Also focus on creating the user flow i.e. the series or steps of actions and tasks that the user took in order to achieve their goal

Mental Models - a mental model is simply a representation of how something works. We cannot keep all of the details of the world in our brains, so we use models to simplify the complex into understandable and organizable chunks.

Artifact Models - a way to capture a set of artifacts that were used to accomplish a goal. Eg: pen & paper to write and a hand kerchief to wipe hands etc.

Physical Models - a way to capture user's environment (focus on what-is-placed-where) to get an idea about the frequency of use of something

  • User flows and storyboards help narrate the current experience that the user is going through along with the ideal flow that the user should have in order to have a better experience
Use Cases are different from storyboards, where the former are specific to the feature of the system i.e. how the system should behave and respond
Eg of a use case: As a shopping-addict, I would like to log in to my online banking account in order to view the balance in my account.

3 Types of scenarios

  1. Context based: explains how best can the product serve the persona
  1. Key-path scenario: a detailed version along with design + system vocab to show how the product can serve the user's needs. Most frequent way the user will take to solve for their goal
  1. Validation scenario: a scenario to test the product against
  • A good way to identify "who" the user is along with their characteristics
  • Helps identify motivations & goals into categories (based on how many personas I have)
  • Can be

    ‚áí behaviour based (eg: repeat users vs one time user)

    ‚áí demography or geography based (north-east culture vs south culture OR male vs female OR teenage vs old people)

    ‚áí emotion based (eg: happy user vs angry/irritated user)

    ‚áí goal based (eg: want the results extremely quick vs take your time but get the correct result)

  • Flow chart of a set of steps the user took when you asked them to perform a task (in Step 1)

  • Scenarios and storyboards help extract design requirements - which are as real as possible
  • Helps convey the potential experience required OR the experience that the user is going through right now
  • It explains "How users accomplish tasks", along with the environmental setting + persona
  • As a <persona>, I need to <requirement>, in order to achieve <goal>
  • Pick the most important use-cases & scenarios (ref to biz goals)

  • Don't create laundry list of features - these are mostly derived without thought for user-needs

üìà Step 3 - Ok, we've prioritised what the user needs, how do we solve for it?

DESIGN the shit out... Start by

  • At a high-level, what will it look like?
  • Think about affordances
  • Define hardware and software requirements etc. (basic form)
  • Inupt methods = how the user will interact with the product

  • The key points that the user is going to interact with. Eg: Persona receives an email and they intend to respond back.
  • Determine the information hierarchy and functional groups

  • Basic structure and positioning of key features
  • Use the wireframe to re-build storyboards like how the user will solve for their goal using feature 1, feature 2 etc.
  • Validate these

  • Bring in brand guidelines
  • Define colour palette + design system definitions
  • Check for competitor brands
  • Images
  • Motions

  • Define what to test. Test for: structure of info & content, naming, effectiveness etc.
  • Usability tests & heuristics (see principles)
